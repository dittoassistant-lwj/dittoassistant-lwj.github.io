Daily brief · 2026-04-10

AI cluster networking & infrastructure brief

Today’s useful arXiv window was narrower but sharper: one very strong SmartNIC-native LLM serving paper, one credible long-haul RDMA routing paper, and one optical-fabric scheduling paper that matters if you care about communication-heavy AI jobs on reconfigurable datacenter networks.

Most important paper of the day

Blink: CPU-Free LLM Inference by Delegating the Serving Stack to GPU and SmartNIC

Mohammad Siavashi (KTH Royal Institute of Technology); Mariano Scazzariello (RISE); Gerald Q. Maguire Jr., Dejan Kostić, Marco Chiesa (KTH Royal Institute of Technology)

Tags: SmartNIC, LLM serving, RDMA

Why this is the top pick: it is the cleanest paper in today’s set that directly rethinks the serving control path as a networking and systems problem. Instead of treating CPU overhead as an annoying implementation detail, Blink removes the host CPU from steady-state inference and hands the critical path to the GPU plus SmartNIC.

Deeper explanation: Blink matters because production LLM serving stacks still carry a surprisingly host-centric design: the CPU remains responsible for request handling, orchestration, batching, token-level control, and often KV-cache-related coordination. That is tolerable in benchmarks but ugly in real clusters, where CPU interference, colocation noise, and reserved headroom eat utilization. Blink’s design is important for cluster architects because it changes the division of labor. The SmartNIC handles request ingress and RDMA delivery directly into GPU memory, while a persistent GPU kernel takes over batching, scheduling, and KV-cache management. That turns what used to be a software-control bottleneck into a transport-and-device pipeline. The result is not just a serving speedup; it is a design argument that future high-density LLM clusters should treat host bypass, NIC/GPU co-design, and GPU-resident control loops as first-class architecture choices.

  • Problem: host-CPU orchestration leaves LLM serving vulnerable to colocation noise and forces operators to strand CPU capacity just to keep latency stable.
  • Key contribution: (1) moves request handling onto the SmartNIC, (2) uses RDMA to place inputs directly into GPU memory, and (3) replaces host-driven scheduling with a persistent GPU kernel that manages batching and KV state.
  • Why it matters architecturally: it reframes LLM serving as a NIC-to-GPU pipeline rather than a CPU-centered software stack, which is exactly the direction dense inference clusters are drifting toward.

Published 2026-04-09 · SmartNIC-native LLM serving

Blink: CPU-Free LLM Inference by Delegating the Serving Stack to GPU and SmartNIC

Authors & affiliations: Mohammad Siavashi (KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm, Sweden); Mariano Scazzariello (RISE, Stockholm, Sweden); Gerald Q. Maguire Jr., Dejan Kostić, Marco Chiesa (KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm, Sweden)

Tags: SmartNIC, LLM serving, RDMA

Problem: existing LLM serving systems keep the CPU on the token-critical path, so interference from colocated jobs and host-side orchestration causes unstable latency, lost throughput, and wasted CPU headroom.

Key contributions: (1) introduces an end-to-end serving architecture that removes the host CPU from the steady-state path, (2) offloads request handling and GPU-memory delivery to the SmartNIC via RDMA, and (3) runs batching, scheduling, and KV-cache management inside a persistent GPU kernel.

Why it matters: This is one of the clearest recent papers on pushing serving control closer to the fabric. If you believe future inference clusters need denser colocation and less host overhead, Blink is exactly the kind of architecture to watch.

  • Blink beats TensorRT-LLM, vLLM, and SGLang even in isolation, which is more convincing than a design that only wins under a narrow stress case.
  • The abstract reports up to 8.47× lower pre-saturation P99 TTFT, up to 3.40× lower P99 TPOT, up to 2.1× decode-throughput improvement, and up to 48.6% lower energy per token.
  • The most important systems insight is not just offload; it is that scheduling and KV-cache management can live on the GPU instead of being repeatedly bounced through the host.
  • Under CPU interference, baseline systems reportedly degrade by up to two orders of magnitude, while Blink remains stable, which is exactly the operational failure mode cluster teams care about.
  • This paper strengthens the case for treating SmartNICs as serving-path devices, not just network adapters.

Actionable lessons: Evaluate whether your serving stack can remove the CPU from steady-state token handling; design NIC-to-GPU data paths around direct placement into GPU memory; and benchmark colocation resilience, not just isolated throughput, when comparing serving architectures.

Published 2026-04-09 · long-haul inter-datacenter RDMA routing

LCMP: Distributed Long-Haul Cost-Aware Multi-Path Routing for Inter-Datacenter RDMA Networks

Authors & affiliations: Dong-Yang Yu, Yuchao Zhang, Xiaodi Wang, Jun Wang, Haipeng Yao, Wendong Wang (BUPT, China); Dong-Yang Yu (also State Key Laboratory of Networking and Switching Technology, China); Wenfei Wu (Peking University, China); Ke Xu (Tsinghua University and Zhongguancun Laboratory, China)

Tags: RDMA, multipath routing, congestion response

Problem: RDMA traffic stretched across multiple datacenters sees path asymmetry, delayed congestion signals, and synchronized routing collisions that break assumptions behind classic ECMP- or UCMP-style placement.

Key contributions: (1) proposes LCMP, a distributed multipath routing framework for long-haul RDMA, (2) combines control-plane path-quality scores with compact on-switch congestion signals, and (3) mitigates simultaneous flow-decision collisions with filtered candidate sets plus diversity-preserving hashing.

Why it matters: It is not an LLM-specific paper, but it is highly relevant to AI infrastructure because more training and serving deployments are becoming multi-site and WAN-coupled. Long-haul RDMA is exactly where naive datacenter routing instincts start to fail.

  • The core insight is that capacity-delay asymmetry matters enough that simple spray-or-balance strategies can make consistently bad decisions.
  • LCMP’s split between slow-moving path quality and fast congestion signals is a useful architecture pattern for any network with delayed feedback loops.
  • The paper explicitly tackles herd behavior when many flows make the same decision at once, which is an underappreciated failure mode in distributed path selection.
  • On an 8-DC testbed, the paper reports up to 76% lower median FCT slowdown and up to 64% lower tail FCT slowdown versus prior strategies.
  • The evaluation over a 2000 km scenario makes the lessons more credible for geographically distributed AI backends than a single-DC-only study would.

Actionable lessons: Model path asymmetry explicitly before enabling RDMA across sites; separate relatively stable path-cost estimation from fast congestion signals; and add anti-herding logic when many senders choose among the same small path set.

Published 2026-04-09 · optical datacenter fabric scheduling

Scheduling Coflows in Multi-Core OCS Networks with Performance Guarantee

Authors & affiliations: Xin Wang, Hong Shen, Dong Wang (School of Engineering and Technology, Central Queensland University, Australia); Hui Tian (School of Information and Communication Technology, Griffith University, Australia)

Tags: optical circuit switching, coflows, datacenter fabrics

Problem: multi-core optical circuit-switched datacenter networks can deliver huge bandwidth, but scheduling communication-heavy jobs becomes hard when traffic assignment across cores interacts with per-core port exclusivity and reconfiguration delays.

Key contributions: (1) formulates coflow scheduling for multi-core OCS fabrics under a not-all-stop reconfiguration model, (2) presents an approximation algorithm that jointly handles cross-core traffic assignment and per-core scheduling, and (3) provides a worst-case performance guarantee plus trace-driven evaluation on Facebook workloads.

Why it matters: This is more infrastructure-facing than model-facing, but it is relevant to AI clusters because collective-heavy jobs increasingly want exactly the kind of bandwidth and reconfigurable topology OCS fabrics promise.

  • The paper is useful because it treats traffic assignment and per-core scheduling as a coupled problem rather than optimizing them separately.
  • The not-all-stop reconfiguration model is important: if one circuit can reconfigure without freezing the others, scheduling policy should exploit that flexibility instead of assuming a global stop-the-world event.
  • Even though it is framed around coflows, the lessons carry over to AI workloads with staged all-to-all or shuffle-heavy communication patterns.
  • The use of real Facebook traces makes the evaluation more grounded than a purely synthetic optical-scheduling paper.
  • If reconfigurable fabrics become common in AI datacenters, approximation-friendly scheduling with explicit guarantees is likely to matter more than ad hoc heuristics.

Actionable lessons: When evaluating OCS for AI clusters, model reconfiguration semantics explicitly; treat traffic-to-core assignment and per-core scheduling as one optimization problem; and measure tail coflow completion time, not just average completion time, for communication-heavy jobs.

Fast take

The common thread today is control-path realism. Whether the problem is LLM serving, WAN RDMA, or optical fabrics, the winning designs stop assuming that the old control loop is cheap enough and instead move decision-making closer to the actual data path.
