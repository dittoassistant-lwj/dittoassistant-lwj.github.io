Daily brief · 2026-04-04

AI cluster networking & infrastructure brief

Today’s set is less about shiny new topologies and more about the operational reality of AI clusters: disaggregated serving starts to fail when KV movement saturates links, production training needs always-on NCCL- and OS-aware diagnosis, and thousand-GPU systems only work when storage, RDMA fabric, and runtime policy are designed as one stack.

Most important paper of the day

Not All Prefills Are Equal: PPD Disaggregation for Multi-turn LLM Serving

Zongze Li, Jingyu Liu, Zach Xu, Tahseen Rabbani, Ce Zhang (University of Chicago, USA); Yineng Zhang (Independent Researcher)

Tags: disaggregated LLM serving, KV-cache transfer, routing policy

Why this is the top pick: it goes straight at one of the most important near-term network problems in LLM infrastructure: multi-turn serving makes prefill/decode disaggregation look clean on paper, but repeated KV movement can quietly turn the fabric into the latency bottleneck.

Deeper explanation: PPD’s core insight is architectural rather than purely scheduling-centric. In multi-turn chat workloads, many later-turn prefills are append-prefills that only process newly arrived tokens while reusing cached KV state. Treating those requests the same way as full prefills forces unnecessary traffic back across the prefill/decode boundary and compounds interference on both the network and the decode workers. PPD instead lets some Turn-2+ work stay local to decode nodes when cached state is already there, then uses a dynamic routing policy to trade TTFT against TPOT under different SLO priorities. For AI cluster designers, that is the interesting part: once serving is disaggregated, request routing, cache placement, and network load are no longer separate layers. They are the same systems problem.

  • Problem: standard prefill/decode disaggregation wastes bandwidth on repeated KV transfers in multi-turn serving and can degrade both latency and service quality under load.
  • Key contribution: PPD dynamically decides when append-prefill work should execute locally on decode nodes instead of traversing the network back to dedicated prefill workers.
  • Why it matters architecturally: it shows that disaggregated serving should be designed around state locality and fabric pressure, not just around nominal phase separation.
  • Useful diagram: the system design figure is the one to study first because it contrasts traditional PD disaggregation with PPD’s local append-prefill path and makes the avoided KV traffic explicit.
Published 2026-03-09 · disaggregated serving over a constrained fabric

Not All Prefills Are Equal: PPD Disaggregation for Multi-turn LLM Serving

Authors & affiliations: Zongze Li, Jingyu Liu, Zach Xu, Tahseen Rabbani, Ce Zhang (University of Chicago, USA); Yineng Zhang (Independent Researcher)

Tags: disaggregated LLM serving, KV-cache transfer, dynamic routing

Problem: classic prefill/decode disaggregation assumes pushing requests between dedicated prefill and decode workers is the right default, but in multi-turn workloads that creates repeated KV transfers and unnecessary network pressure.

Key contributions: (1) identifies append-prefill as a materially different operation from full prefill, (2) proposes Prefill Prefill-capable Decode disaggregation so Turn-2+ append-prefill can stay local to decode nodes when cached state is already present, and (3) introduces a weighted routing policy that adapts to different SLO priorities instead of hard-coding one strategy.

Why it matters: this paper is a sharp reminder that the fabric cost of state movement now shapes LLM serving architecture. Once KV state is distributed, routing policy is effectively a network-control mechanism.

  • Multi-turn serving breaks the assumption that all prefills are equally disruptive.
  • Bandwidth spent on repeated KV handoff can dominate the latency story even when GPU compute is nominally the main resource.
  • No single routing policy is best across TTFT- vs. TPOT-sensitive deployments, so operators need explicit control knobs.
  • State locality on decode nodes is an asset that should be exploited rather than overwritten by a rigid phase split.
  • Disaggregation works best when cache reuse and network pressure are modeled together.

Actionable lessons: separate append-prefill from full-prefill in serving telemetry; track KV-transfer bandwidth as a first-class saturation metric; and design routing policies that can favor locality when link pressure rises instead of always enforcing centralized prefill.

Useful diagram: the PPD architecture and routing-path figure is especially useful because it makes clear which request paths consume extra network bandwidth and which stay on-node with cached KV state.

Published 2026-03-31 · production-scale AI training diagnosis

SysOM-AI: Continuous Cross-Layer Performance Diagnosis for Production AI Training

Authors & affiliations: Yusheng Zheng, Wenan Mao, Shuyi Cheng, Fuqiu Feng, Guangshui Li, Zhaoyan Liao, Yongzhuo Huang, Zhenwei Xiao, Yuqing Li, Tao Ma (Alibaba Group, China); Andi Quinn (UC Santa Cruz, USA)

Tags: NCCL observability, cross-layer diagnosis, AI training operations

Problem: production AI training failures often emerge as subtle OS or runtime issues that cascade into stalled collectives and network slowdowns, but existing tools usually only expose one layer at a time or impose too much overhead to run continuously.

Key contributions: (1) introduces a layered differential diagnosis method that compares stragglers across ranks and against historical baselines, (2) combines CPU stack profiling, GPU kernel tracing, and NCCL event instrumentation with adaptive hybrid unwinding and eBPF-based tracing, and (3) demonstrates always-on deployment across more than 80,000 GPUs with under 0.4% overhead.

Why it matters: fabric-aware cluster design is not just about transport and topology anymore. At real scale, the hard part is telling whether a slowdown came from the host, the runtime, the collective layer, or the network—and doing that fast enough to matter.

  • Collective slowdowns are often symptoms, not root causes.
  • Per-communication-group diagnosis is more useful than node-level averages for distributed training.
  • Always-on observability only works if data collection overhead is pushed low enough for production use.
  • Historical baselines are essential because uniform degradations can hide when no single node looks obviously bad.
  • AI-cluster operations increasingly need cross-layer evidence, not isolated network counters.

Actionable lessons: instrument NCCL and host behavior together; retain per-rank and per-communication-group baselines for incident response; and treat low-overhead production tracing as control-plane infrastructure, not a debugging afterthought.

Useful diagram: the system overview linking CPU profiling, GPU tracing, NCCL observability, and differential diagnosis is the key figure because it shows how evidence flows across layers to isolate the root cause of training slowdowns.

Published 2026-03-18 · thousand-GPU RDMA training infrastructure

Thousand-GPU Large-Scale Training and Optimization Recipe for AI-Native Cloud Embodied Intelligence Infrastructure

Authors & affiliations: Chenfeng Gu, Chen Zhou, Haoran Li, Haoran Sun, Hedan Yang, Jing Long, Junwu Xiong, Luqiao Wang, Mingxi Luo, Qiming Yang, Shuai Di, Song Wang, Tianyun Zhao, Wanting Xu, Wen Huang, Xiaodong Bai, Xiaomeng Tian, Xiaolong Xiang, Yicheng Gong, Yongjian Guo, Yucheng Guo, Yuzhen Li, Yunxuan Ma, Yu Wei, Zhong Guan, Zhen Sun (AI Infra Team at JDT; with coauthor affiliations spanning Tsinghua University, Peking University, Tianjin University, Beihang University, and the University of Science and Technology of China)

Tags: RDMA fabric, thousand-GPU training, storage-network-compute co-design

Problem: embodied-AI training at thousand-GPU scale stresses the entire system stack at once, from data flow and storage throughput to RDMA transport and distributed runtime behavior.

Key contributions: (1) reports an industry deployment recipe for thousand-GPU embodied-intelligence training, (2) pairs model- and sequence-level optimization with infrastructure work including high-performance storage, a 3.2T RDMA network, and a Ray-driven elastic AI data lake, and (3) demonstrates a major reduction in single-round training time for the GR00T-N1.5 model.

Why it matters: even though the paper is broader than networking alone, it is useful because it treats the network as one component of a coupled training substrate rather than as an isolated fabric that can be tuned after the fact.

  • At thousand-GPU scale, data-path design and communication-path design are inseparable.
  • RDMA capacity only pays off if storage and runtime layers can feed it consistently.
  • Large speedups often come from stacking many modest systems optimizations rather than one silver-bullet fabric trick.
  • Embodied-AI workloads create pressure on both training throughput and data-ingest elasticity.
  • Closed-loop evaluation matters because infrastructure changes can easily move bottlenecks rather than remove them.

Actionable lessons: capacity-plan storage, RDMA, and data-lake orchestration together; validate training speedups against end-to-end round time instead of kernel-level wins; and keep infrastructure recipes tied to workload characteristics rather than generic “GPU cluster” heuristics.

Useful diagram: the end-to-end infrastructure overview is the figure to study because it shows how the data layer, storage system, RDMA network, and training framework are coupled into one embodied-intelligence platform.

Updated 2026-03-09 · online troubleshooting in production clusters

EROICA: Online Performance Troubleshooting for Large-Scale Model Training

Authors & affiliations: Yu Guan, Zhiyu Yin, Haoyu Chen, Sheng Cheng, Chaojie Yang, Kun Qian, Pengcheng Zhang, Yang Zhang, Hanyu Zhao, Yong Li, Dennis Cai, Ennan Zhai (Alibaba Cloud); Tianyin Xu (University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign)

Tags: cluster troubleshooting, online profiling, distributed training

Problem: large-model training incidents are hard to localize in production because hardware faults, software issues, and communication anomalies can all manifest as the same symptom: slower training progress at scale.

Key contributions: (1) presents an online troubleshooting system that combines fine-grained profiling with full-cluster coverage, (2) summarizes runtime behavior patterns and uses differential observability to localize root causes with low production impact, and (3) reports deployment across roughly 100,000 GPUs for 1.5 years with high diagnosis success.

Why it matters: EROICA complements more network-centric papers by showing the operational machinery needed once training clusters are already in production. A fast fabric is only useful if you can tell when it stopped being the bottleneck and something else became the limiting factor.

  • Production troubleshooting needs both coverage and resolution; sacrificing either one misses real incidents.
  • Differential observability is a practical way to isolate anomalies without fully reproducing failures offline.
  • Large AI clusters need diagnosis systems designed for continuous use, not occasional deep dives.
  • The line between systems monitoring and performance engineering is disappearing in model-training operations.
  • Root-cause localization improves when the tool understands training structure rather than just generic host metrics.

Actionable lessons: build troubleshooting paths that compare ranks, jobs, and time windows instead of relying on absolute thresholds alone; preserve enough runtime context to distinguish communication symptoms from true fabric faults; and treat online profiling as part of cluster reliability engineering.

Useful diagram: the architecture figure that ties runtime profiling, observability summarization, and differential diagnosis together is the most useful because it shows how the system scales incident analysis to full production clusters.

Fast take

The through-line today is operational realism. AI infrastructure is reaching the point where state locality, KV movement, NCCL behavior, RDMA capacity, and low-overhead diagnosis all have to be designed together. The “network paper” is no longer just about congestion control; it is increasingly about how the whole cluster notices, routes around, and reasons about communication pressure.
